Mike Stebbins: Projects

Thesis Title: Obtaining Material Properties of the Plantar Soft Tissue for a Patient-
Specific Finite Element Model of the Foot

If you don’t currently know someone who has diabetes, you will soon: 1/3 of American adults will have the disease by 2050.  Currently, people with diabetes make up only 8% of the US population, but they undergo 60% of all non-traumatic lower limb amputations.  That’s 66,000 amputations per year, or 180 per day.
 
As a Type 1 diabetic, I have a special interest in the work that my group does.  My research deals with compressively loading a subject's foot with a custom-designed, computer-controlled, MRI-compliant loading device while 3D images of the foot tissues are obtained via gated MR imaging.  An inverse finite element model is solved with the force vs. deformation data to find patient-specific material properties.  We will use the models of various diabetic and control subjects to understand the stresses that develop inside of diabetic feet, stresses that lead to foot ulcers and freqently, amputations.

Currently, the device is built, undergoing bench-top testing, and has just recently become patent-pending.  The video on the left shows the foot loading platen successfully moving for the first time (the sine wave displacement makes it look a little slow at the ends of the movement, but it's moving as it should).  The image on the right is the poster that I presented at the 2011 Northwest Biomechanics Symposium in Vancouver, B.C., which won the Best Poster award.  Click on it for an easier-to-read pdf.
