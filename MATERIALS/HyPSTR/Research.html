Mike Stebbins: Projects

Thesis Title: Obtaining Material Properties of the Plantar Soft Tissue for a Patient-
Specific Finite Element Model of the Foot


If you don’t currently know someone who has diabetes, you will soon: the CDC estimates that 1/3 of American adults will have the disease by 2050.  People with diabetes make up only 8% of the US population, but they undergo 60% of all non-traumatic lower limb amputations.  That’s 66,000 amputations per year, or 180 per day.
 
As a Type 1 diabetic, I have a special interest in the work that our Veteran's Affairs Center of Excellence group does.  My research deals with loading a subject's foot using a custom-designed, computer-controlled, MRI-compliant loading device while 3D images of the foot tissues are obtained.  An inverse finite element model is solved with the resulting force vs. deformation data to find patient-specific material properties.  We will use the information from both diabetic and non-diabetic subjects to better understand the higher stresses that develop inside of the diabetic foot, which cause foot ulcers and all-too-freqently, amputations.

The loading device is currently undergoing testing and is already patent-pending. 




This video shows a quick overview of some of the device hardware followed by a short displacement test.  You can see how the loading platen will push on the test subjects' foot while they lie inside the MRI.

This is the slave assembly.  The piston is attached to a loading platen (maroon-brown) that displaces the foot.  This image also shows the LVDT (orange) used to accurately measure the displacement of the piston, and the custom-designed mounting/adjustment mechanism that ensures the LVDT is aligned with the piston.  The piston, cylinder, platen and mounting plates were designed by another engineer in our group, while I was responsible for mounting and aligning the LVDT.
